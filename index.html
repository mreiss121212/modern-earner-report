02 — Independent Earner Portrait
70 million Americans
who earn on their own terms.
Independent Earners aren't a monolith — they're gig workers, freelancers, casual sellers, and business side-hustlers who share one defining trait: they generate income outside the traditional employer-employee relationship.
Most don't do this instead of a job. They do it alongside one.
~58M Independent Earners layer independent income on top of traditional employment — a full-time job plus gig work, a freelance client roster, or a side business selling products. Only ~12M earn exclusively through independent work. This means their financial complexity isn't the absence of a paycheck; it's the addition of multiple, irregular income streams on top of one.

Independent Earners are defined broadly: anyone earning via gig platforms, freelance or professional services, informal services (babysitting, lawn care, repairs), business or product sales, or casual selling. The Q1 2026 definition expanded to include casual selling, which partly explains the growth from 54M to 70M vs Q4 2024.

03 — Hourly Worker Portrait
79 million workers
paid by the hour.
Hourly workers span industries — retail, food service, healthcare, transportation, construction, logistics. What unites them is how income works: not a fixed salary but hours logged, shifts scheduled, and tips earned. That unpredictability creates financial lives that look very different from their salaried peers.
Their income isn't stable — it's scheduled. And schedules change.
Unlike Independent Earners whose variability comes from irregular freelance work, Hourly Workers experience variability through the mechanics of employment itself — shift changes, cut hours, seasonal slowdowns, tips that fluctuate with traffic. A slow week or a shift that gets canceled changes everything for someone without a salary cushion.

This makes them Cash App's most naturally aligned segment. 57% already have a Cash App account — the highest account ownership of any adult segment. They're already in the ecosystem. The gap is converting that account ownership to primary banking status, where the core value of instant access and cash management can fully land.

Hourly Workers are also the segment under the most acute financial stress: 38% run out of money before their next paycheck at least half the time — higher than both Independent Earners and Salary Workers. This drives materially higher usage of Cash App Borrow (22%) and free overdraft coverage (15%) compared to salary earners.

12 — Interpretation
What the data
means — and doesn't.
Key themes from the Q1 2026 findings and the strategic context for reading this data carefully.
Finding 01
Modern Earning is mainstream, not marginal.
At 67% of working-age adults, Modern Earners are not a niche demographic. The high incidence across all income tiers ($30–70K: 73%; $70K+: 62%) means this is a structural feature of the American labor market — not a low-income or gig-specific phenomenon. Implication: Products designed for Modern Earners are not serving a segment; they are serving the majority.
Finding 02
Income complexity is the defining variable — not income level.
The data shows that income variability (83% of IEs, 53% of Hourly Workers) and multi-source earning (94% of IEs have 2+ streams annually) are the primary drivers of financial complexity — more so than income amount. Implication: Financial products that manage predictability, timing, and flow — not just amount — are most relevant to this population. The 38M gap (MAU to primary) likely reflects friction in cash-flow management, not product awareness.
Finding 03
The quarter-over-quarter shifts are directional, not definitive.
The apparent growth in Independent Earners (54M → 70M) and the increase in Cash App primary banking (4% → 12%) are notable but should be interpreted carefully. These are spot comparisons between independent samples, not a panel. The IE definition also expanded in Q1 2026 to include casual selling. Changes may reflect real shifts in the population, methodological differences, or both. Implication: Use these figures to understand directional momentum, not to calculate precise growth rates.
Finding 04
The teen cohort is a durable entry point, not just a size story.
A 31% primary rate among teen Cash App account holders — vs 10% for adults — is not simply a higher number. It reflects that when teens adopt Cash App during their first banking years, it tends to become central to their financial life. Implication: Teen acquisition economics are meaningfully different from adult acquisition; the lifetime value calculation favors early engagement disproportionately.
Data Limitations to Keep in Mind
All income and banking data is self-reported — subject to recall and social desirability bias. "Primary bank" reflects self-designation, which may differ from transactional primacy (where paycheck is deposited, where most spending occurs). Segment counts are population estimates with sampling error; treat as directional rather than precise. Q4 2024–Q1 2026 comparisons are not panel-based — do not interpret as individual-level change. Feature adoption data reflects past-12-month use among account holders; does not imply current active use.
13 — Methodology
How the data
was collected.
A full account of study design, sampling, definitions, and analytical approach for the Q1 2026 study and the Q1 2026 vs Q4 2024 comparisons.
Study Design
Study Name
Earner Sizing Refresh Study — Q1 2026. Conducted by Cash App Customer Intelligence & Strategy.
Study Type
Online quantitative survey. Cross-sectional design — independent sample at each wave. Not a longitudinal panel.
Q1 2026 Sample
n = 3,495 (weighted: 3,482). Ages 15–64 with personal income. Fielded Q1 2026.
Q4 2024 Sample
n = 4,148. Ages 18–64 with personal income. Fielded Q4 2024. Note: Q4 2024 did not include ages 15–17; teen comparisons are Q1 2026 only.
HHI Supplement
Household income segment analysis from the HHI Refresh Study (Q1 2026). Breakdowns by <$30K / $30–70K / $70K+ HHI.
Segment Definitions
Modern Earner (NET)
Any individual age 15–64 who qualifies as an Independent Earner, Hourly Worker, or Teen Earner. Segments are not mutually exclusive. Business owners without other qualifying income are excluded.
Independent Earners
Adults earning via gig platforms, freelance, informal services, business ownership, or casual selling — whether primary or supplemental. Q1 2026 expanded to include casual selling (not in Q4 2024 definition).
Hourly Workers
All hourly-compensated employees — full-time and part-time. Q1 2026 includes part-time hourly (Q4 2024 was full-time only); the full-time-only count was ~42M in Q1 2026 and ~45M in Q4 2024.
Salary Earners
Full-time salaried employees. Included as a comparison group; not part of the Modern Earner definition. ~46M in Q1 2026.
Teen Earners
Ages 15–17 with any income in the past 12 months. Q1 2026 only (not included in Q4 2024 design).
Statistical Approach
Significance Testing
Statistical significance tested at p = 0.05 using False Discovery Rate (FDR) correction to control for multiple comparisons. ↑/↓ symbols in source data denote statistically significant differences vs the comparison group.
Population Sizing
Segment sizes estimated by applying survey incidence rates to US Census population estimates for ages 15–64. These are model-based estimates with sampling error; reported figures are rounded to nearest million.
Wave Comparisons
Cross-wave comparisons are descriptive, not inferential. Different sample sizes, fielding periods, and (in some cases) definition changes mean wave-over-wave differences cannot be tested for statistical significance directly. Treat as indicative.
Data Sources & Scope
Primary Source
All data in this report sourced exclusively from the Earner Sizing Refresh Study (Q1 2026) and the HHI Refresh Study (Q1 2026), unless explicitly noted as Q4 2024.
Gen Pop Benchmark
The "Working-Age Pop." column in the Data Explorer reflects all survey respondents ("Total" column), including non-Modern-Earners. It serves as a working-age population proxy, not a nationally representative benchmark.
Financial Data
All banking and financial data is self-reported. Account ownership reflects past-12-month holding or use. "Primary bank" reflects respondent self-designation. Feature adoption reflects past-12-month use among Cash App account holders.
